# Chapter 4: Descent and Dust

The landing pad was a cracked concrete scar in the middle of a sprawling rust desert. Prefabricated habitation modules—stark, hexagonal metal boxes—clustered around the pad, all stained the same pervasive, bleeding orange. The sky above was a thin, sickly pink, dominated by a sun that looked small, pale, and distant.

Franze stepped off the ramp first, immediately securing his respirator mask over his nose and mouth. The air here was bitter. Even through the filter, he tasted iron and ozone. 

K followed, moving with an eerie grace in the bulky survival suit. She didn't blink against the harsh, flat light. 

They were assigned to Hab-Module 4—a claustrophobic tin can containing two narrow bunks, a microscopic sanitation unit, and a central terminal for uploading field data. Franze tossed his duffel onto the left bunk. K took the right. 

The first two weeks were a grinding, monotonous routine designed to break the spirit. Morning check-ins at the central lab. Afternoons spent in heavy rovers, driving out to the sprawling lichen fields to take core samples of the frozen, modified dirt. Evenings eating synthetic protein paste in the mess hall, surrounded by the paranoid whispers of other neutral crews.

Franze tried to bridge the gap. He offered observations about the weather, complaints about the food, small pieces of his own life—how he missed the smell of real ocean water, how he ended up pushing papers instead of holding a rifle. 

K offered nothing in return. She was a ghost haunting the hab-module. 

But Franze, trained as a logistics officer, noticed anomalies. He noticed the discrepancies in her routine. 

She never spoke of Earth. She never received comm-bursts from family. More importantly, she logged excessive hours at the central terminal in their room. Franze would wake in the middle of the night to the soft, blue glow of the screen, K sitting in the chair, her back rigidly facing him, her fingers flying across the haptic keyboard at a speed impossible for standard data entry.

And once, during a particularly vicious dust storm that kept them locked inside for two days, he woke up to find her holding something small and frayed. A photograph, hidden in the lining of her heavy surface boot. When she realized he was awake, it vanished with the speed of a magician's trick.

He didn’t ask. But the seed of suspicion had taken root.
