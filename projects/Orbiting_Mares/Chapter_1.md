# Chapter 1: The Writer’s Room

She closed the leather-bound notebook, the finality of the thud echoing in the small, cluttered study. Outside, the Seattle rain tapped against the reinforced glass of her window—a soft, persistent rhythm that usually coaxed the words from her mind. Tonight, it only served as a metronome for her failure. 

Her name was Mira. For three agonizing months, she had been wrestling with the same story, caught in an endless loop of drafting, deleting, and despairing. The characters felt like ghosts refusing to materialize, their voices muffled by the fog of her own hesitation.

“Dinner’s cold,” David’s voice drifted from the kitchen doorway. 

“I’m not hungry,” she replied, not turning around. 

David walked in anyway, the scent of garlic and reheated marinara following him. He carried two bowls of pasta, setting one down on the edge of her desk, carefully avoiding the precarious stacks of historical texts and star charts. He pulled up a wooden chair, straddled it backward, and pointed a fork at the closed notebook.

“Let me hear it,” he said gently.

Mira hesitated. She traced the embossed spine of the notebook. It wasn’t just a story. It was a haunting. 

“It’s not working,” she whispered. “The pacing is wrong. The boy, Franze, he’s too rigid. And K… she’s just a cipher.”

“Read it,” David insisted, taking a bite of his pasta. “You’re too close to it. Let me be the audience.”

Mira sighed, opening to the first page. The ink was dark, the handwriting sharp and jagged. She took a breath, letting the ambient sound of the rain fade, and began to read, projecting herself across time and the vacuum of space, back to a year she was trying so desperately to forget.
