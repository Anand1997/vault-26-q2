# Chapter 8: The Unwritten Truth

Mira closed the notebook, her hand trembling slightly. 

The rain was still falling in Seattle. The pasta in David’s bowl was gone. He sat perfectly still, staring at her across the cluttered desk. 

“That’s it?” he asked softly. “They just run into the dust?”

“The storm hides their heat signatures,” Mira said, her voice thick. “They make it to a smuggler's ship hidden in the northern canyons. They get off-world.”

David leaned forward, resting his elbows on his knees. “It’s brilliant, Mira. It really is. The tension, the atmosphere... but it feels... unresolved. Who was Franze really? And K... she’s so vivid. She feels real.”

Mira looked down at her hands. The knuckles were white, just like Franze's had been on the yoke of the rover. 

“You said earlier that I was hiding something,” Mira said quietly. 

“I said Franze needed a flaw,” David corrected gently. 

“No. You said I was hiding something. In the story, and in real life.”

Mira reached down to the bottom drawer of her desk. It was locked. She pulled a small key from a chain around her neck, unlocked the drawer, and pulled out a small, frayed photograph. 

She slid it across the desk. 

David picked it up. He looked at the image of the young girl standing between the doctor and the scientist. Then he looked up at his wife. He looked at her sharp features, her dark, unblinking eyes. 

“Mira?” he whispered.

“My name is Kavya,” she said, the words feeling like glass in her throat. “Franze died three years ago. The radiation from the Belt finally caught up with him. My parents are gone. The kill-codes died with them. I am the only one left.”

David stared at her, the world shifting on its axis. “You wrote it as fiction… because…”

“Because if I write it as history, the Fleet will find me,” Kavya said. “Because if I don't write it at all, the ghosts win. I had to write it. I had to let him live again on the page.”

She pulled the notebook back toward her. She picked up her pen, the ink dark and heavy. 

She turned to a fresh, blank page. 

*For Franze,* she wrote. *Who chose chaos. Who chose me.*

She closed the notebook for the final time. The story was over. But for the first time in ten years, the storm inside her head had finally broken.
