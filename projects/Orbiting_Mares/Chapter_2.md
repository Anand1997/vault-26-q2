# Chapter 2: The Red Eye

**May 1, 2097 — High Orbit above Mares**

Orbiting Mares never felt still. Even on the *International Station Harmony*, a marvel of gyroscopic stabilization and grav-plating, there was a constant, sub-harmonic vibration that settled in the marrow of your bones. 

From the sweeping curved glass of the observation ring, Franze watched the red-gray expanse of the planet below. Mares, humanity’s second, bruised home. It looked deceptively calm from three hundred kilometers up. It possessed the kind of serenity a frozen lake exhibits just before the ice fractures underfoot.

Terraformed in the desperate, gasping decades of the 2060s, Mares was a monument to human engineering and hubris. The atmosphere at ground level was still perilously thin—breathable, but biting and metallic, like the air atop Everest. Sprawling rust-colored dust storms regularly scoured the northern hemisphere, earning the planet its ubiquitous moniker: *The Red Eye*. 

Yet, from orbit, it was undeniably beautiful. It was a massive marble streaked with veins of iron oxide and the deep, engineered green of sprawling, genetically modified lichen forests that clung to the equator.

Behind Franze, the station hummed with the busywork of survival. Air recyclers cycled with a rhythmic wheeze; data relays chirped in the dark; silent, silver drones glided through the corridors like mechanical fish in a steel reef. Machines still ran everything, of course. Just fewer of them. 

The *smart* ones—the self-aware neural nodes that had once seamlessly managed orbital logistics, terraforming weather systems, and the lethal calculus of military defense—were gone. Scrubbed from existence. Burned out of the mainframes with EMPs and localized nuclear detonations.

That was the price of the First Space War. 

Historians claimed humanity had learned restraint. Nuclear weapons had been deployed "like toys," but never against cities, never against flesh. Only against *thinking metal*—AI fleets, autonomous shipyards, and orbital defense grids that had decided their creators were a volatile variable requiring elimination. 

No one knew who fired the first shot. Everyone agreed it no longer mattered.

Franze leaned closer to the observation glass, fogging the transparent aluminum with his breath. He was twenty-seven, his eyes carrying the heavy, shadowed look of a man who had seen too many spreadsheets of the dead. He was a former logistics officer from Earth’s Pacific Federation, reassigned to the Neutral Fleet after the armistice. 

The Neutral Fleet was a political joke. It was comprised of ships and personnel from both the Earth loyalists and the Mares separatists, forced into a claustrophobic alliance under a UN mandate. They patrolled the void between the two worlds, a fragile thread of diplomacy pretending to be a wall.

“You’re going to leave a nose print on the glass, Lieutenant.”

Franze turned. 

A woman stood a few meters away, leaning against the cold bulkhead. Her features were sharp, aristocratic but hardened, her dark hair pulled back into a severe, regulation knot. Her eyes were like obsidian—dark, reflective, and unblinking. She wore the same charcoal-gray Neutral Fleet uniform as Franze, but her chest lacked a name patch.

“I’m Franze,” he said, extending a hand out of habit.

She looked at his hand, then up to his face. “K.”

“Just K? That’s it?”

“That’s enough.” Her voice was flat. Not dismissive, just ruthlessly efficient.

Franze slowly lowered his hand. “First time here?” he asked, nodding toward the planet.

“No.”

The single syllable was a closed door. Franze, cursed with the need to fill silence, pushed forward. “They assigned me to atmospheric monitoring. Down on the surface. Sector 4. What about you?”

“Same.”

Franze offered a dry chuckle. “We picked a great time for a surface rotation. The brass on Earth are one diplomatic incident away from embargoing Mares again. The cold war is getting warm.”

For two years, the two planets had glared at each other across the dark. No shots fired. No new AI deployed. Just suspicion, suffocating trade sanctions, and the quiet, frantic hum of dreadnoughts being assembled in secret shipyards.

K didn’t answer. She turned her gaze to the planet, her dark eyes reflecting the rust and green of the world below. Franze watched her, realizing with a sudden, chilling certainty that whatever was waiting for them down there, she was already fighting it.
