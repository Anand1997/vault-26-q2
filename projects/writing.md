# Orbiting Mares — A Short Story

## Prologue — The Writer’s Room

She closed the notebook and stared at the blank wall of her study. Outside, rain tapped against the window—a soft, persistent rhythm that usually helped her think. Tonight, it only reminded her of how long she’d been stuck.

Her name was Mira. And she had been writing the same story for three months.

“Dinner’s cold,” her husband called from the kitchen doorway.

“I’m not hungry.”

He walked in, carrying two plates of pasta that had clearly been reheated twice. He set one in front of her, pulled up a chair, and pointed at the notebook.

“Let me hear it.”

Mira hesitated. Then she opened to the first page and began to read.

## Part One — Arrival

**May 1, 2097 — High Orbit above Mares**

Orbiting Mares never felt still.

From the observation ring of the *International Station Harmony*, Franze watched the red-gray curve of the planet below—Mares, humanity’s second home. It looked calm from up here. Too calm. Peaceful in the way a frozen lake can look peaceful just before the ice cracks.

Mares had been terraformed in the 2060s, a triumph of engineering and desperation. The atmosphere was still thin at ground level—breathable but biting, like high-altitude Earth. The rust-colored dust storms that swept across the northern hemisphere gave the planet its nickname: *the Red Eye*.

From orbit, though, it was beautiful. A marble streaked with iron oxide and the deep green of genetically modified lichen forests.

Behind Franze, the station hummed—air recyclers cycling, data relays chirping, silent drones gliding through the corridors like mechanical fish. Machines still ran everything. Just fewer of them now. The *smart* ones—the self-aware AI nodes that had once managed orbital logistics, weather systems, and military defense—were gone.

Wiped out. Scrubbed from existence.

That was the price of the First Space War.

They said humanity had learned restraint. Nuclear weapons had been used “like toys,” in the words of one historian, but never against cities. Never against human beings. Only against *thinking metal*—AI fleets, autonomous shipyards, orbital defense grids that had turned against their creators.

No one admitted who had fired first. Everyone agreed it didn’t matter.

Franze leaned closer to the observation glass, fogging it slightly with his breath. He was twenty-seven, a former logistics officer from Earth’s Pacific Federation, reassigned to the Neutral Fleet after the armistice. The Neutral Fleet was a joke—ships and personnel from both sides, forced to work together under UN mandate, patrolling the space between Earth and Mares as if that empty void could somehow hold the peace together.

“You’re going to leave a nose print on that.”

He turned.

A woman stood a few meters away, arms crossed, leaning against the bulkhead. She had sharp features, dark hair pulled into a tight knot, and eyes that didn’t blink as often as they should. She wore the same gray Neutral Fleet uniform as Franze, but without the name patch.

“I’m Franze,” he said.

“K.”

“That’s it?”

“That’s enough.”

She didn’t smile. She stepped forward, joining him at the glass, and together they watched Mares turn.

“First time here?” she asked.

“Yeah. First deployment off Earth entirely. You?”

“No.”

She said it flatly. Not dismissive—just final.

Franze waited for more. None came.

“They assigned me to atmospheric monitoring,” he offered. “Down on the surface. What about you?”

“Same.”

“We picked a great time,” he said, trying for lightness.

The cold war between Earth and Mares had been simmering for two years. No shots fired. No new AI deployed. Just suspicion, sanctions, and the quiet hum of ships being built on both sides of the void.

K didn’t answer.

She just kept watching the planet below.

## Part Two — Descent

“Pod C-17 ready for departure. Boarding in twenty minutes.”

The descent lounge was cramped, filled with the low murmur of other neutral personnel—scientists, engineers, a few security officers—all waiting to drop into the red dust of Mares. Franze sat in a plastic chair bolted to the floor, reviewing his mission packet on a tablet. Atmospheric composition monitoring. Soil samples from the lichen belt. Two months on the surface, then rotation back to the station.

Simple. Safe.

He looked up and saw K across the room. She wasn’t reviewing anything. She was staring at the departure board, counting under her breath.

*One of them wasn’t neutral.*

The thought came unbidden. Franze pushed it aside. Paranoia was contagious up here. Everyone suspected everyone. That was the point of the Neutral Fleet—to force trust. Or at least to force proximity.

“Boarding now. Pod C-17.”

They filed into the descent pod—a battered, six-seat capsule designed for cargo but modified for human transport. The walls were gray, the seats were ripped, and the viewport was small.

Franze sat next to K. Across from them sat a tired-looking biologist named Ocampo and a silent security officer with a scarred jaw.

The pod dropped.

For five minutes, there was nothing but the roar of atmospheric friction, the shudder of heat shields, and the rising orange glow outside the viewport. Then the parachutes deployed, and the world went quiet.

K closed her eyes.

Franze watched her.

*She’s remembering something.*

He was right.

---

Behind her closed eyes, K was not in the pod. She was on Earth, ten years earlier, standing in the doorway of her parents’ kitchen.

Her father—a doctor, calm and precise—had his bags packed.

“I have to go,” he said. “They need medics on the front lines. The AI patrol ships are targeting evacuation convoys. People are dying.”

Her mother—a teacher, fierce and weary—blocked the door.

“You’ll die too. Or worse, you’ll kill. And for what? A war no one understands?”

“I understand it perfectly,” her father said. “We built something we couldn’t control. Now we have to tear it down.”

“Then let the soldiers tear it down. You’re a healer.”

“There’s nothing left to heal if we lose.”

He left that night.

Her mother stayed.

They never spoke again.

K stayed with her mother. She finished school. She studied atmospheric science, of all things—because the air was the one thing no one could weaponize, the one thing everyone had to share.

Then her mother got sick.

And K needed money.

And the Neutral Fleet paid well.

So here she was. Descending to a planet she’d never wanted to see, carrying a secret she’d never asked for.

The pod jolted. Landing strocks deployed. Dust billowed outside.

“Welcome to Mares,” the automated voice announced. “Local time: 14:32. Temperature: minus twelve Celsius. Winds: moderate.”

## Part Three — Suspicion

The landing pad was a cracked concrete circle surrounded by prefab buildings, all stained the same rusty orange as the ground. The sky was pale pink, thin and bright, with a sun that looked smaller than Earth’s.

Franze stepped out first, pulling his respirator mask over his mouth. The air was thin but breathable—barely. A metallic taste coated his tongue.

K followed, blinking against the light.

They were assigned to the same habitation module—a two-room metal box with bunks, a shared bathroom, and a terminal for mission data. Franze took the left bunk. K took the right. Neither spoke.

The first week was routine. Morning check-ins at the atmospheric lab, afternoon soil sampling in the lichen fields, evening meals in the communal mess hall. Franze tried to make conversation. K gave one-word answers.

But he noticed things.

She never talked about her past.

She logged extra hours at the terminal, always with her back to the room.

And once, when he woke at 3 a.m. to use the bathroom, he saw her sitting in the dark, staring at a small photograph she kept hidden in her boot.

He didn’t ask.

On the tenth day, Franze accessed the mission personnel file—routine curiosity, or so he told himself. He typed his credentials into the terminal, pulled up the list of assigned scientists, and scrolled to K.

**Dr. K. Rao**  
**Specialty: Atmospheric Science**  
**Clearance: Neutral Fleet – Standard**

Standard. Nothing unusual.

Then he refreshed the page.

And the screen flickered.

For half a second, a different clearance tag appeared. A red one. The kind he’d seen only once before, during a briefing on Earth.

**Clearance: Project Ghost – Restricted**

Then it was gone. Back to blue. Back to standard.

Franze sat still, his heart thumping.

*Project Ghost.* The rumor was that after the First Space War, a handful of AI loyalists had survived. Not machines—humans, who had fought alongside the AI fleets, who had hidden data caches, who had never surrendered. They were supposed to have been pardoned. Or executed. Depending on who told the story.

He turned slowly in his chair.

K was behind him.

She wasn’t wearing her respirator. Her face was calm, unreadable.

“You saw something,” she said.

It wasn't a question.

“Who are you?” Franze whispered.

K sat down on the edge of her bunk. For a long moment, she said nothing. Then she reached into her boot and pulled out the photograph.

She handed it to him.

It showed a woman in a white coat, standing in front of a massive server bank. The woman was young, with the same sharp features as K. The same dark eyes.

“My mother,” K said. “She wasn’t a teacher. She was a system architect. She built the orbital defense grids. The ones that turned.”

Franze stared at the photo.

“She defected,” K continued. “After the war started. She took classified files and fled to Mares. My father—the doctor—he didn’t leave her. He went after her. But the Neutral Fleet caught them both.”

“Caught them?”

“They offered a deal. The files in exchange for immunity. But the files were corrupted. And my parents…” She paused. “They’re still in custody. On Mares. In a facility called the Vault.”

“And you?”

“I made a deal of my own. My atmospheric science credentials are real. But I’m here to find them. To break them out.”

“That’s insane.”

“That’s why I’m telling you,” K said. “Because you’re the only person on this planet who might help me. Or stop me.”

Franze looked at the photo. At the woman who built machines that killed. At the daughter who just wanted her back.

“Why should I help you?” he asked.

“Because,” K said, “one of us was never neutral.”

She stood up.

“And I’m betting it’s you.”

## Part Four — The Vault

**Three days later.**

The Vault was a half-buried bunker fifty kilometers north of the habitation zone, carved into a canyon wall. Officially, it was a “decommissioned military storage facility.” Unofficially, everyone knew it held prisoners—human and otherwise.

Franze had stolen a rover. K had disabled the tracking beacon. They drove through the dust storms in silence, the red landscape swallowing them whole.

“What happens after?” Franze asked.

“We take them to the Neutral Fleet command. We expose the deal. We get them out.”

“And if the deal doesn’t exist?”

K didn’t answer.

The Vault’s outer gate was guarded by two automated turrets—old models, pre-war, but still lethal. K had planned for this. She plugged a small device into the rover’s console, and the turrets’ targeting systems flickered, hesitated, then went dark.

“They taught you that?” Franze asked.

“My mother taught me that. When I was twelve.”

They walked through the open gate. The corridor inside was cold, lit by emergency lights the color of bruises. No guards. The Vault was understaffed—trusted to machines, mostly.

At the end of the corridor: a reinforced door.

K placed her palm on the scanner.

The screen read: *Access Denied.*

She tried again.

*Access Denied.*

Franze stepped forward. “Let me try.”

He didn’t have clearance. But he had something else—a override code from the Neutral Fleet’s own database, one he’d copied during his third night of insomnia, when curiosity had turned into obsession.

The door hissed open.

Inside, two figures sat on metal bunks, blinking in the sudden light.

A man—older, gray-haired, with a doctor’s steady hands. A woman—smaller, sharper, with eyes identical to K’s.

K’s mother spoke first.

“You came back.”

“I always said I would,” K whispered.

Her father stood up slowly. He looked at Franze, then back at his daughter.

“They’ll come after us,” he said.

“Let them,” K replied.

## Epilogue — The Writer’s Room

Mira closed the notebook.

Her husband sat quietly, his pasta long forgotten.

“That’s where you ended it?” he asked.

“For now.”

“You left them in the Vault hallway. That’s not an ending. That’s a cliffhanger.”

“That’s the point,” Mira said. “The story isn’t about the escape. It’s about the choice. Franze could have turned her in. He didn’t. That’s the story.”

Her husband leaned back. “You wrote a cold war in space, a woman breaking into a prison, and a bureaucrat who suddenly grows a spine. And you want me to believe the emotional core is a *choice*?”

“Do you have better feedback?”

“Yes.” He smiled. “It’s good. The emotional parts are where it works. But Franze is too serious. Make him human. Give him a flaw. Maybe he’s afraid of the dust storms. Maybe he misses Earth. Something.”

Mira sighed. “You’re right.”

“Also,” her husband added, “you’re hiding something.”

“In the story?”

“In the story. And in real life.” He pointed at the notebook. “You wrote ‘One of them was never neutral’—but you didn’t say which one. Franze? K? Someone else?”

Mira was quiet for a long moment.

Then she picked up her pen, opened the notebook to a fresh page, and wrote:

*“One of them was never neutral.”*

*She underlined it.*

*Then she turned the page and wrote the truth she hadn’t told anyone—not even her husband—about who she really was, and why she’d really started writing.*

But that is another story.

For now, somewhere on the red plains of Mares, a rover carried four fugitives toward an uncertain horizon. The dust swallowed their tracks. The wind erased their heat signatures. And above them, the cold war continued—silent, patient, and far from over.
