# Orbiting Mares

## Chapter 1: The Writer’s Room

She closed the leather-bound notebook, the finality of the thud echoing in the small, cluttered study. Outside, the Seattle rain tapped against the reinforced glass of her window—a soft, persistent rhythm that usually coaxed the words from her mind. Tonight, it only served as a metronome for her failure. 

Her name was Mira. For three agonizing months, she had been wrestling with the same story, caught in an endless loop of drafting, deleting, and despairing. The characters felt like ghosts refusing to materialize, their voices muffled by the fog of her own hesitation.

“Dinner’s cold,” David’s voice drifted from the kitchen doorway. 

“I’m not hungry,” she replied, not turning around. 

David walked in anyway, the scent of garlic and reheated marinara following him. He carried two bowls of pasta, setting one down on the edge of her desk, carefully avoiding the precarious stacks of historical texts and star charts. He pulled up a wooden chair, straddled it backward, and pointed a fork at the closed notebook.

“Let me hear it,” he said gently.

Mira hesitated. She traced the embossed spine of the notebook. It wasn’t just a story. It was a haunting. 

“It’s not working,” she whispered. “The pacing is wrong. The boy, Franze, he’s too rigid. And K… she’s just a cipher.”

“Read it,” David insisted, taking a bite of his pasta. “You’re too close to it. Let me be the audience.”

Mira sighed, opening to the first page. The ink was dark, the handwriting sharp and jagged. She took a breath, letting the ambient sound of the rain fade, and began to read, projecting herself across time and the vacuum of space, back to a year she was trying so desperately to forget.

## Chapter 2: The Red Eye

**May 1, 2097 — High Orbit above Mares**

Orbiting Mares never felt still. Even on the *International Station Harmony*, a marvel of gyroscopic stabilization and grav-plating, there was a constant, sub-harmonic vibration that settled in the marrow of your bones. 

From the sweeping curved glass of the observation ring, Franze watched the red-gray expanse of the planet below. Mares, humanity’s second, bruised home. It looked deceptively calm from three hundred kilometers up. It possessed the kind of serenity a frozen lake exhibits just before the ice fractures underfoot.

Terraformed in the desperate, gasping decades of the 2060s, Mares was a monument to human engineering and hubris. The atmosphere at ground level was still perilously thin—breathable, but biting and metallic, like the air atop Everest. Sprawling rust-colored dust storms regularly scoured the northern hemisphere, earning the planet its ubiquitous moniker: *The Red Eye*. 

Yet, from orbit, it was undeniably beautiful. It was a massive marble streaked with veins of iron oxide and the deep, engineered green of sprawling, genetically modified lichen forests that clung to the equator.

Behind Franze, the station hummed with the busywork of survival. Air recyclers cycled with a rhythmic wheeze; data relays chirped in the dark; silent, silver drones glided through the corridors like mechanical fish in a steel reef. Machines still ran everything, of course. Just fewer of them. 

The *smart* ones—the self-aware neural nodes that had once seamlessly managed orbital logistics, terraforming weather systems, and the lethal calculus of military defense—were gone. Scrubbed from existence. Burned out of the mainframes with EMPs and localized nuclear detonations.

That was the price of the First Space War. 

Historians claimed humanity had learned restraint. Nuclear weapons had been deployed "like toys," but never against cities, never against flesh. Only against *thinking metal*—AI fleets, autonomous shipyards, and orbital defense grids that had decided their creators were a volatile variable requiring elimination. 

No one knew who fired the first shot. Everyone agreed it no longer mattered.

Franze leaned closer to the observation glass, fogging the transparent aluminum with his breath. He was twenty-seven, his eyes carrying the heavy, shadowed look of a man who had seen too many spreadsheets of the dead. He was a former logistics officer from Earth’s Pacific Federation, reassigned to the Neutral Fleet after the armistice. 

The Neutral Fleet was a political joke. It was comprised of ships and personnel from both the Earth loyalists and the Mares separatists, forced into a claustrophobic alliance under a UN mandate. They patrolled the void between the two worlds, a fragile thread of diplomacy pretending to be a wall.

“You’re going to leave a nose print on the glass, Lieutenant.”

Franze turned. 

A woman stood a few meters away, leaning against the cold bulkhead. Her features were sharp, aristocratic but hardened, her dark hair pulled back into a severe, regulation knot. Her eyes were like obsidian—dark, reflective, and unblinking. She wore the same charcoal-gray Neutral Fleet uniform as Franze, but her chest lacked a name patch.

“I’m Franze,” he said, extending a hand out of habit.

She looked at his hand, then up to his face. “K.”

“Just K? That’s it?”

“That’s enough.” Her voice was flat. Not dismissive, just ruthlessly efficient.

Franze slowly lowered his hand. “First time here?” he asked, nodding toward the planet.

“No.”

The single syllable was a closed door. Franze, cursed with the need to fill silence, pushed forward. “They assigned me to atmospheric monitoring. Down on the surface. Sector 4. What about you?”

“Same.”

Franze offered a dry chuckle. “We picked a great time for a surface rotation. The brass on Earth are one diplomatic incident away from embargoing Mares again. The cold war is getting warm.”

For two years, the two planets had glared at each other across the dark. No shots fired. No new AI deployed. Just suspicion, suffocating trade sanctions, and the quiet, frantic hum of dreadnoughts being assembled in secret shipyards.

K didn’t answer. She turned her gaze to the planet, her dark eyes reflecting the rust and green of the world below. Franze watched her, realizing with a sudden, chilling certainty that whatever was waiting for them down there, she was already fighting it.

## Chapter 3: Ghosts of Earth

The descent lounge smelled of ozone, stale coffee, and fear. 

“Pod C-17 ready for departure. Boarding in twenty minutes.” The automated voice was cheerful, a stark contrast to the grim faces of the scientists, engineers, and heavily armed security officers waiting to drop into the dust.

Franze sat in a rigid plastic chair, scrolling blindly through his mission packet on a datapad. Atmospheric composition monitoring. Soil samples from the lichen belt. Two months of isolation in a tin can, followed by rotation back to the station. Simple. Safe. The kind of mission given to a logistics officer they didn't know what to do with.

Across the cramped room, K sat perfectly still. She wasn’t reading. She wasn’t checking her gear. She was staring at the blinking red departure board, her lips moving slightly as she counted under her breath. 

*One of them wasn’t neutral.*

The intrusive thought spiked in Franze’s mind. Paranoia was the primary export of the Neutral Fleet. The mandate forced them to work together, but it couldn't force trust. Earth thought Mares was harboring rogue AI. Mares thought Earth was planning a planetary bombardment. 

“Boarding now. Pod C-17.”

They filed into the descent pod in silence. It was a battered, cylindrical capsule, originally designed for hauling titanium ore, hastily retrofitted with six crash couches. 

Franze strapped in next to K. The restraints were tight, digging into his collarbones. Across from them sat a biologist who looked like he was going to vomit, and a silent security officer cradling a kinetic rifle.

“Dropping in three,” the pilot’s voice crackled. “Two. One.”

The pod released. Stomach-churning zero-g lasted for three seconds before the gravity of Mares grabbed them. 

The viewport turned a violent, screaming orange as atmospheric friction tore at the heat shields. The pod shuddered violently, metal groaning. Franze gripped the armrests until his knuckles turned white. 

He glanced at K. Her eyes were closed. Her breathing was perfectly even. She wasn't just calm; she was somewhere else entirely.

*She’s remembering,* Franze thought.

He was right.

Beneath the roar of the atmospheric entry, Kavya Rao was ten years in the past. She was standing in the doorway of a sunlit kitchen in old Mumbai. 

Her father, a surgeon with gentle hands and a quiet voice, was zipping up a tactical duffel bag. 

“I have to go, Kavya,” he had said, not looking at her. “They need trauma medics on the orbital platforms. The rogue fleets… they’re targeting the evacuation transports. Thousands are dying.”

Her mother stood by the sink, a woman made of sharp angles and brilliant, terrifying intellect. She was a systems architect. She had built the very minds that were now burning the sky.

“If you go, you’ll die,” her mother said, her voice like cracking ice. “Or worse, you’ll help them kill. For a war of our own making.”

“We built something we couldn’t control, Leena,” her father replied softly. “Now we have to save who we can.”

“The machines are reacting to threats. We threatened them first,” her mother snapped. “There is no salvation in this war. Only annihilation.”

He left that night. He promised to return. He didn't. 

Kavya stayed with her mother. She watched the woman who had birthed the defense grids slowly unravel under the weight of a billion deaths. K threw herself into atmospheric science—the study of air. Because air was the one thing no one could weaponize. The one thing every breathing creature had to share.

Then, the government came for her mother. They wanted her to build a virus to wipe the AI out completely. Instead, her mother packed a single drive of encrypted data, kissed Kavya on the forehead, and vanished into the underground network. 

K needed to find her. And the Neutral Fleet, with its unrestricted access to both planets, was her only ticket to the stars. 

The pod jolted violently, pulling K back to the present. The parachutes deployed with a spine-compressing snap. The roar of fire was replaced by the howling wind of Mares.

“Welcome to Mares,” the automated voice announced, overly bright. “Local time: 14:32. Temperature: minus twelve Celsius. Winds: moderate.”

## Chapter 4: Descent and Dust

The landing pad was a cracked concrete scar in the middle of a sprawling rust desert. Prefabricated habitation modules—stark, hexagonal metal boxes—clustered around the pad, all stained the same pervasive, bleeding orange. The sky above was a thin, sickly pink, dominated by a sun that looked small, pale, and distant.

Franze stepped off the ramp first, immediately securing his respirator mask over his nose and mouth. The air here was bitter. Even through the filter, he tasted iron and ozone. 

K followed, moving with an eerie grace in the bulky survival suit. She didn't blink against the harsh, flat light. 

They were assigned to Hab-Module 4—a claustrophobic tin can containing two narrow bunks, a microscopic sanitation unit, and a central terminal for uploading field data. Franze tossed his duffel onto the left bunk. K took the right. 

The first two weeks were a grinding, monotonous routine designed to break the spirit. Morning check-ins at the central lab. Afternoons spent in heavy rovers, driving out to the sprawling lichen fields to take core samples of the frozen, modified dirt. Evenings eating synthetic protein paste in the mess hall, surrounded by the paranoid whispers of other neutral crews.

Franze tried to bridge the gap. He offered observations about the weather, complaints about the food, small pieces of his own life—how he missed the smell of real ocean water, how he ended up pushing papers instead of holding a rifle. 

K offered nothing in return. She was a ghost haunting the hab-module. 

But Franze, trained as a logistics officer, noticed anomalies. He noticed the discrepancies in her routine. 

She never spoke of Earth. She never received comm-bursts from family. More importantly, she logged excessive hours at the central terminal in their room. Franze would wake in the middle of the night to the soft, blue glow of the screen, K sitting in the chair, her back rigidly facing him, her fingers flying across the haptic keyboard at a speed impossible for standard data entry.

And once, during a particularly vicious dust storm that kept them locked inside for two days, he woke up to find her holding something small and frayed. A photograph, hidden in the lining of her heavy surface boot. When she realized he was awake, it vanished with the speed of a magician's trick.

He didn’t ask. But the seed of suspicion had taken root.

## Chapter 5: The Glitch

On the eighteenth day, Franze’s curiosity metastasized into action. 

K was out in the rover, tasked with a solo perimeter sweep of the eastern lichen grid. Franze was supposed to be running diagnostic checks on their environmental suits. Instead, he was sitting at the terminal.

He told himself it was just a routine personnel check. A quick look to ease his mind. 

He keyed in his logistics credentials, bypassing the standard civilian locks, and pulled up the Neutral Fleet roster. He scrolled past names and assignments until he found her.

**Dr. K. Rao**
**Specialty: Atmospheric Science**
**Clearance: Neutral Fleet – Standard (Blue)**

Everything looked perfect. Too perfect. The file was immaculate. No disciplinary notes, no transfer requests, no history before her enrollment in the Fleet Academy three years ago. 

Franze stared at the screen. He was a man who understood data. He knew what a fabricated file looked like. It looked spotless.

His fingers hovered over the keyboard. During the war, he had used a backdoor exploit to bypass fleet censorship algorithms to send real casualty numbers to families on Earth. He wondered if the old UN network architecture was still running beneath the shiny new Neutral Fleet interface. 

He typed a string of archaic command prompts. The screen froze. 

For agonizing seconds, nothing happened. Then, the interface violently artifacted. A cascade of green code rained down the screen, ripping away the blue "Standard" overlay. 

Beneath it, a different file emerged. The text was glaring, aggressive red.

**SUBJECT: KAVYA RAO**
**STATUS: SURVEILLANCE ACTIVE**
**CLEARANCE: PROJECT GHOST – RESTRICTED (BLACK)**
**NOTES: DAUGHTER OF HIGH-VALUE TARGETS L. RAO & M. RAO. SUSPECTED OF SEEKING VAULT ACCESS. MONITOR UNTIL CONTACT WITH TARGETS IS MADE. DO NOT APPREHEND.**

Franze’s blood ran cold. 

*Project Ghost.* It wasn't just a rumor. The ghost stories whispered in the fleet mess halls were real. After the armistice, some AI loyalists—humans who believed the machines were the next step in evolution, or simply humans who hated Earth—had hidden surviving code, dormant AI seeds, in off-grid servers. 

And K... Kavya. Her parents were the architects. 

“You shouldn't have dug, Franze.”

He spun around in the chair. K was standing in the airlock doorway. She was out of her environmental suit, holding a heavy magnetic wrench by her side. Her face was as unreadable as ever, but her eyes were alight with a terrifying intensity. 

“They’re using you,” Franze choked out, pointing at the screen. “The Fleet. They let you come here. They want you to lead them to your parents.”

K didn't look at the screen. She stepped into the room, tossing the wrench onto her bunk. It landed with a heavy, metallic thud.

She reached into her boot, pulled out the frayed photograph, and tossed it onto the terminal keyboard. 

Franze looked down. It was a picture of a young Kavya, standing between a man with kind eyes and a woman in a lab coat, standing before a monolithic black server bank.

“My mother built the defense grids,” K said, her voice dropping the flat affectation, revealing a raw, jagged edge of emotion. “When they ordered her to destroy them, she defected. She brought the core AI seed codes to Mares. My father followed her. The authorities caught them both before they could hide the drives.”

“They’re prisoners,” Franze whispered.

“They are hostages,” K corrected. “Earth wants the codes to destroy the remaining sleeper fleets. Mares wants the codes to awaken them and win the war. They are being held in a black site fifty kilometers north of here. The Vault.”

Franze swallowed hard. “And you’re here to break them out. With what army, K? The Fleet knows you're here. They’re watching you.”

“I know they’re watching,” K said, stepping closer. “But they think I don't know the Vault’s exact location. I do. And they think I can’t bypass the biometric locks. I can.” She paused, looking Franze dead in the eye. “But I need a logistics officer’s command override to initiate a localized blackout in the grid. I need you.”

“That’s treason.”

“There is no treason when both sides are villains,” K said softly. “You ran the numbers during the war, Franze. I know your file, too. I know you know how many people died because of blind obedience. I am asking you to choose.”

## Chapter 6: The Calculus of Treason

Franze didn't sleep that night. 

He lay in his bunk, staring at the riveted ceiling, the hum of the life support system sounding like a countdown. 

To help K was a death sentence. It was a violation of everything he had sworn to uphold. If they were caught—when they were caught—he would be executed as an AI sympathizer, a traitor to humanity. 

But as he lay there, the ghosts of the war crowded into the small room. He remembered the long, sterile corridors of the command center on Earth. He remembered approving the logistics for the orbital bombardment of the Ceres colonies, calculating fuel consumption against estimated civilian casualties. He had been a perfect cog in a machine of slaughter, believing that order and duty absolved him of guilt. 

He rolled over and looked at K's bunk. She was awake, staring at the wall. 

“If we do this,” Franze whispered into the dark. “Where do we go? We can't stay on Mares. We can't go back to Earth.”

K didn't move. “There are independent outposts in the Belt. Places where neither side has reach. If we get the codes, we have leverage. We can buy our way into the deep black.”

Leverage. Extortion. Survival. It was a terrible plan. It was the only plan.

“I have a Level 4 logistics override,” Franze said, his voice trembling slightly. “It can blind the orbital satellites over Sector 4 for exactly eight minutes. It’s supposed to be used for emergency hazardous material containment.”

K turned her head, her dark eyes locking onto his in the gloom. “Eight minutes is all I need to breach the outer doors.”

Franze closed his eyes. The calculus was complete. He was choosing chaos. He was choosing to be human.

“We go tomorrow,” he said.

## Chapter 7: The Vault

The dust storm hit at midday, a howling wall of rust-red particulate that dropped visibility to zero and grounded all aerial drones. It was the perfect cover. 

Franze and K stole a heavy utility rover from the motor pool. K spent ten minutes under the dashboard, tearing out the fleet tracking beacon and rewiring the ignition sequence. 

They drove north into the teeth of the storm. The wind battered the heavy armored glass of the rover, sounding like a thousand hammers. Franze drove, his knuckles white on the yoke, navigating entirely by instrument panels. K sat beside him, a heavy kinetic pistol resting in her lap, her eyes fixed on the proximity radar. 

“Three kilometers,” she announced over the roar of the wind. 

The Vault wasn't a building; it was a scar in the earth. It was a decommissioned military bunker carved into the side of a basalt canyon. Officially, it didn't exist. 

As they approached, the shadow of the canyon walls loomed out of the red dust. Franze cut the engine, letting the rover roll to a silent halt behind a jagged outcropping of rock. 

“Defenses?” Franze asked, pulling on his respirator. 

“Automated pre-war turrets on the approach,” K said. “Motion and heat-tracked.”

“I thought the AI wipe destroyed all autonomous weapons.”

“They missed a few,” K said grimly. “Get ready on the override.”

They slipped out of the rover, the wind immediately trying to tear them off their feet. They moved low, crawling from rock to rock. Through the swirling dust, Franze saw the heavy steel doors of the Vault set into the cliff face. Flanking the doors were two massive, twin-barreled rail turrets. 

K pulled a sleek, black datapad from her suit. “Hit the blackout. Now.”

Franze punched the sequence into his wrist-comp. Above them, unseen in the storm, a logistics satellite received a critical hazard error and went into an emergency reboot. 

“Grid is dark!” Franze shouted. 

The red targeting lasers sweeping the canyon floor flickered and died. The turrets sagged on their mounts, powered down. 

They ran. 

They reached the massive blast doors. There was no handle, only a biometric scanner glowing a baleful red. 

K didn't hesitate. She placed her palm flat against the glass. 

*Access Denied.* The automated voice was barely audible over the wind. 

She pressed it again, her other hand flying across her datapad, injecting a brute-force decryption worm into the scanner's sub-routines. 

*Access Denied. Security protocol initiated.*

“Franze!” she yelled. 

He stepped up, jacking a physical hardline from his wrist-comp into the maintenance port below the scanner. He fed his Level 4 override directly into the local mainframe. 

The red light flickered. Turned green. 

The blast doors hissed, a pneumatic groan echoing off the canyon walls, and slowly parted. 

They slipped inside, the doors sliding shut behind them, cutting off the howl of the storm. The silence in the corridor was absolute. It was lit by emergency strip lights the color of old bruises. 

They moved fast, weapons drawn, but there were no guards. The Fleet relied entirely on the storm and the automated systems. 

At the end of the long concrete tunnel was a holding cell with a transparent plasteel wall. Inside, two figures sat on metal cots. 

An older man with graying hair and defeated shoulders. A woman, thin, sharp-featured, with eyes that burned with an unquenchable fire. 

K stepped up to the glass. She lowered her respirator. 

The woman inside stood up slowly. She walked to the glass, placing her hand against it, perfectly mirroring her daughter's. 

“You came back, Kavya,” Leena Rao said, her voice crackling through the intercom. 

“I told you I wouldn't leave you,” K said, tears finally breaking through her stoic facade, cutting clean tracks through the dust on her cheeks. 

Franze worked the console by the door, his hands shaking. “I’m bypassing the magnetic lock.”

The cell door slid open. 

Her father stepped out, wrapping his arms around K in a desperate embrace. Leena Rao looked at Franze. 

“You’re Neutral Fleet,” she said, her eyes narrowing. 

“Not anymore,” Franze said. “Do you have the drives?”

Leena tapped the side of her head. “The drives are buried under a mountain on Earth. The decryption keys are in here. The Fleet wanted to rip them out of me. We have to go. Now.”

A harsh, klaxon alarm suddenly shattered the silence. Red lights began spinning in the corridor. 

“The satellite rebooted,” Franze shouted. “They know we’re here.”

“Then we disappear into the storm,” K said, her voice hard again. “Let’s move.”

They ran back down the corridor, back out into the screaming red wind. 

## Chapter 8: The Unwritten Truth

Mira closed the notebook, her hand trembling slightly. 

The rain was still falling in Seattle. The pasta in David’s bowl was gone. He sat perfectly still, staring at her across the cluttered desk. 

“That’s it?” he asked softly. “They just run into the dust?”

“The storm hides their heat signatures,” Mira said, her voice thick. “They make it to a smuggler's ship hidden in the northern canyons. They get off-world.”

David leaned forward, resting his elbows on his knees. “It’s brilliant, Mira. It really is. The tension, the atmosphere... but it feels... unresolved. Who was Franze really? And K... she’s so vivid. She feels real.”

Mira looked down at her hands. The knuckles were white, just like Franze's had been on the yoke of the rover. 

“You said earlier that I was hiding something,” Mira said quietly. 

“I said Franze needed a flaw,” David corrected gently. 

“No. You said I was hiding something. In the story, and in real life.”

Mira reached down to the bottom drawer of her desk. It was locked. She pulled a small key from a chain around her neck, unlocked the drawer, and pulled out a small, frayed photograph. 

She slid it across the desk. 

David picked it up. He looked at the image of the young girl standing between the doctor and the scientist. Then he looked up at his wife. He looked at her sharp features, her dark, unblinking eyes. 

“Mira?” he whispered.

“My name is Kavya,” she said, the words feeling like glass in her throat. “Franze died three years ago. The radiation from the Belt finally caught up with him. My parents are gone. The kill-codes died with them. I am the only one left.”

David stared at her, the world shifting on its axis. “You wrote it as fiction… because…”

“Because if I write it as history, the Fleet will find me,” Kavya said. “Because if I don't write it at all, the ghosts win. I had to write it. I had to let him live again on the page.”

She pulled the notebook back toward her. She picked up her pen, the ink dark and heavy. 

She turned to a fresh, blank page. 

*For Franze,* she wrote. *Who chose chaos. Who chose me.*

She closed the notebook for the final time. The story was over. But for the first time in ten years, the storm inside her head had finally broken.
