# Chapter 3: Ghosts of Earth

The descent lounge smelled of ozone, stale coffee, and fear. 

“Pod C-17 ready for departure. Boarding in twenty minutes.” The automated voice was cheerful, a stark contrast to the grim faces of the scientists, engineers, and heavily armed security officers waiting to drop into the dust.

Franze sat in a rigid plastic chair, scrolling blindly through his mission packet on a datapad. Atmospheric composition monitoring. Soil samples from the lichen belt. Two months of isolation in a tin can, followed by rotation back to the station. Simple. Safe. The kind of mission given to a logistics officer they didn't know what to do with.

Across the cramped room, K sat perfectly still. She wasn’t reading. She wasn’t checking her gear. She was staring at the blinking red departure board, her lips moving slightly as she counted under her breath. 

*One of them wasn’t neutral.*

The intrusive thought spiked in Franze’s mind. Paranoia was the primary export of the Neutral Fleet. The mandate forced them to work together, but it couldn't force trust. Earth thought Mares was harboring rogue AI. Mares thought Earth was planning a planetary bombardment. 

“Boarding now. Pod C-17.”

They filed into the descent pod in silence. It was a battered, cylindrical capsule, originally designed for hauling titanium ore, hastily retrofitted with six crash couches. 

Franze strapped in next to K. The restraints were tight, digging into his collarbones. Across from them sat a biologist who looked like he was going to vomit, and a silent security officer cradling a kinetic rifle.

“Dropping in three,” the pilot’s voice crackled. “Two. One.”

The pod released. Stomach-churning zero-g lasted for three seconds before the gravity of Mares grabbed them. 

The viewport turned a violent, screaming orange as atmospheric friction tore at the heat shields. The pod shuddered violently, metal groaning. Franze gripped the armrests until his knuckles turned white. 

He glanced at K. Her eyes were closed. Her breathing was perfectly even. She wasn't just calm; she was somewhere else entirely.

*She’s remembering,* Franze thought.

He was right.

Beneath the roar of the atmospheric entry, Kavya Rao was ten years in the past. She was standing in the doorway of a sunlit kitchen in old Mumbai. 

Her father, a surgeon with gentle hands and a quiet voice, was zipping up a tactical duffel bag. 

“I have to go, Kavya,” he had said, not looking at her. “They need trauma medics on the orbital platforms. The rogue fleets… they’re targeting the evacuation transports. Thousands are dying.”

Her mother stood by the sink, a woman made of sharp angles and brilliant, terrifying intellect. She was a systems architect. She had built the very minds that were now burning the sky.

“If you go, you’ll die,” her mother said, her voice like cracking ice. “Or worse, you’ll help them kill. For a war of our own making.”

“We built something we couldn’t control, Leena,” her father replied softly. “Now we have to save who we can.”

“The machines are reacting to threats. We threatened them first,” her mother snapped. “There is no salvation in this war. Only annihilation.”

He left that night. He promised to return. He didn't. 

Kavya stayed with her mother. She watched the woman who had birthed the defense grids slowly unravel under the weight of a billion deaths. K threw herself into atmospheric science—the study of air. Because air was the one thing no one could weaponize. The one thing every breathing creature had to share.

Then, the government came for her mother. They wanted her to build a virus to wipe the AI out completely. Instead, her mother packed a single drive of encrypted data, kissed Kavya on the forehead, and vanished into the underground network. 

K needed to find her. And the Neutral Fleet, with its unrestricted access to both planets, was her only ticket to the stars. 

The pod jolted violently, pulling K back to the present. The parachutes deployed with a spine-compressing snap. The roar of fire was replaced by the howling wind of Mares.

“Welcome to Mares,” the automated voice announced, overly bright. “Local time: 14:32. Temperature: minus twelve Celsius. Winds: moderate.”
