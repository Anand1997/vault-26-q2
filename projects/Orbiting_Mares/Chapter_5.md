# Chapter 5: The Glitch

On the eighteenth day, Franze’s curiosity metastasized into action. 

K was out in the rover, tasked with a solo perimeter sweep of the eastern lichen grid. Franze was supposed to be running diagnostic checks on their environmental suits. Instead, he was sitting at the terminal.

He told himself it was just a routine personnel check. A quick look to ease his mind. 

He keyed in his logistics credentials, bypassing the standard civilian locks, and pulled up the Neutral Fleet roster. He scrolled past names and assignments until he found her.

**Dr. K. Rao**
**Specialty: Atmospheric Science**
**Clearance: Neutral Fleet – Standard (Blue)**

Everything looked perfect. Too perfect. The file was immaculate. No disciplinary notes, no transfer requests, no history before her enrollment in the Fleet Academy three years ago. 

Franze stared at the screen. He was a man who understood data. He knew what a fabricated file looked like. It looked spotless.

His fingers hovered over the keyboard. During the war, he had used a backdoor exploit to bypass fleet censorship algorithms to send real casualty numbers to families on Earth. He wondered if the old UN network architecture was still running beneath the shiny new Neutral Fleet interface. 

He typed a string of archaic command prompts. The screen froze. 

For agonizing seconds, nothing happened. Then, the interface violently artifacted. A cascade of green code rained down the screen, ripping away the blue "Standard" overlay. 

Beneath it, a different file emerged. The text was glaring, aggressive red.

**SUBJECT: KAVYA RAO**
**STATUS: SURVEILLANCE ACTIVE**
**CLEARANCE: PROJECT GHOST – RESTRICTED (BLACK)**
**NOTES: DAUGHTER OF HIGH-VALUE TARGETS L. RAO & M. RAO. SUSPECTED OF SEEKING VAULT ACCESS. MONITOR UNTIL CONTACT WITH TARGETS IS MADE. DO NOT APPREHEND.**

Franze’s blood ran cold. 

*Project Ghost.* It wasn't just a rumor. The ghost stories whispered in the fleet mess halls were real. After the armistice, some AI loyalists—humans who believed the machines were the next step in evolution, or simply humans who hated Earth—had hidden surviving code, dormant AI seeds, in off-grid servers. 

And K... Kavya. Her parents were the architects. 

“You shouldn't have dug, Franze.”

He spun around in the chair. K was standing in the airlock doorway. She was out of her environmental suit, holding a heavy magnetic wrench by her side. Her face was as unreadable as ever, but her eyes were alight with a terrifying intensity. 

“They’re using you,” Franze choked out, pointing at the screen. “The Fleet. They let you come here. They want you to lead them to your parents.”

K didn't look at the screen. She stepped into the room, tossing the wrench onto her bunk. It landed with a heavy, metallic thud.

She reached into her boot, pulled out the frayed photograph, and tossed it onto the terminal keyboard. 

Franze looked down. It was a picture of a young Kavya, standing between a man with kind eyes and a woman in a lab coat, standing before a monolithic black server bank.

“My mother built the defense grids,” K said, her voice dropping the flat affectation, revealing a raw, jagged edge of emotion. “When they ordered her to destroy them, she defected. She brought the core AI seed codes to Mares. My father followed her. The authorities caught them both before they could hide the drives.”

“They’re prisoners,” Franze whispered.

“They are hostages,” K corrected. “Earth wants the codes to destroy the remaining sleeper fleets. Mares wants the codes to awaken them and win the war. They are being held in a black site fifty kilometers north of here. The Vault.”

Franze swallowed hard. “And you’re here to break them out. With what army, K? The Fleet knows you're here. They’re watching you.”

“I know they’re watching,” K said, stepping closer. “But they think I don't know the Vault’s exact location. I do. And they think I can’t bypass the biometric locks. I can.” She paused, looking Franze dead in the eye. “But I need a logistics officer’s command override to initiate a localized blackout in the grid. I need you.”

“That’s treason.”

“There is no treason when both sides are villains,” K said softly. “You ran the numbers during the war, Franze. I know your file, too. I know you know how many people died because of blind obedience. I am asking you to choose.”
