# Chapter 7: The Vault

The dust storm hit at midday, a howling wall of rust-red particulate that dropped visibility to zero and grounded all aerial drones. It was the perfect cover. 

Franze and K stole a heavy utility rover from the motor pool. K spent ten minutes under the dashboard, tearing out the fleet tracking beacon and rewiring the ignition sequence. 

They drove north into the teeth of the storm. The wind battered the heavy armored glass of the rover, sounding like a thousand hammers. Franze drove, his knuckles white on the yoke, navigating entirely by instrument panels. K sat beside him, a heavy kinetic pistol resting in her lap, her eyes fixed on the proximity radar. 

“Three kilometers,” she announced over the roar of the wind. 

The Vault wasn't a building; it was a scar in the earth. It was a decommissioned military bunker carved into the side of a basalt canyon. Officially, it didn't exist. 

As they approached, the shadow of the canyon walls loomed out of the red dust. Franze cut the engine, letting the rover roll to a silent halt behind a jagged outcropping of rock. 

“Defenses?” Franze asked, pulling on his respirator. 

“Automated pre-war turrets on the approach,” K said. “Motion and heat-tracked.”

“I thought the AI wipe destroyed all autonomous weapons.”

“They missed a few,” K said grimly. “Get ready on the override.”

They slipped out of the rover, the wind immediately trying to tear them off their feet. They moved low, crawling from rock to rock. Through the swirling dust, Franze saw the heavy steel doors of the Vault set into the cliff face. Flanking the doors were two massive, twin-barreled rail turrets. 

K pulled a sleek, black datapad from her suit. “Hit the blackout. Now.”

Franze punched the sequence into his wrist-comp. Above them, unseen in the storm, a logistics satellite received a critical hazard error and went into an emergency reboot. 

“Grid is dark!” Franze shouted. 

The red targeting lasers sweeping the canyon floor flickered and died. The turrets sagged on their mounts, powered down. 

They ran. 

They reached the massive blast doors. There was no handle, only a biometric scanner glowing a baleful red. 

K didn't hesitate. She placed her palm flat against the glass. 

*Access Denied.* The automated voice was barely audible over the wind. 

She pressed it again, her other hand flying across her datapad, injecting a brute-force decryption worm into the scanner's sub-routines. 

*Access Denied. Security protocol initiated.*

“Franze!” she yelled. 

He stepped up, jacking a physical hardline from his wrist-comp into the maintenance port below the scanner. He fed his Level 4 override directly into the local mainframe. 

The red light flickered. Turned green. 

The blast doors hissed, a pneumatic groan echoing off the canyon walls, and slowly parted. 

They slipped inside, the doors sliding shut behind them, cutting off the howl of the storm. The silence in the corridor was absolute. It was lit by emergency strip lights the color of old bruises. 

They moved fast, weapons drawn, but there were no guards. The Fleet relied entirely on the storm and the automated systems. 

At the end of the long concrete tunnel was a holding cell with a transparent plasteel wall. Inside, two figures sat on metal cots. 

An older man with graying hair and defeated shoulders. A woman, thin, sharp-featured, with eyes that belonged to a predator.

K stepped up to the glass. She lowered her respirator. 

The woman inside stood up slowly. She walked to the glass, placing her hand against it, perfectly mirroring her daughter's. 

“You came back, Kavya,” Leena Rao said, her voice crackling through the intercom. 

“I told you I wouldn't leave you,” K said, tears finally breaking through her stoic facade, cutting clean tracks through the dust on her cheeks. 

Franze worked the console by the door, his hands shaking. “I’m bypassing the magnetic lock.”

The cell door slid open. 

Her father stepped out, wrapping his arms around K in a desperate embrace. Leena Rao looked at Franze. 

“You’re Neutral Fleet,” she said, her eyes narrowing. 

“Not anymore,” Franze said. “Do you have the drives?”

Leena tapped the side of her head. “The drives are buried under a mountain on Earth. The decryption keys are in here. The Fleet wanted to rip them out of me. We have to go. Now.”

A harsh, klaxon alarm suddenly shattered the silence. Red lights began spinning in the corridor. 

“The satellite rebooted,” Franze shouted. “They know we’re here.”

“Then we disappear into the storm,” K said, her voice hard again. “Let’s move.”

They ran back down the corridor, back out into the screaming red wind.
